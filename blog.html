Career Journey

Welcome to the Curiously Breezy Side of Things

From spreadsheet battles to a blinking cursor of newsletter dread. Discover the unexpected origins of this blog...

There was a time—a couple of years back—when I found myself breaking into a polite panic over an empty Word document, a blinking cursor mocking me in rhythm. I wasn’t writing code or crafting blog posts back then.
I was writing a newsletter—yes, one of those email missives meant to inform, inspire, and occasionally sweet-talk you into showing up at one of our trainings.

It was called the "KARDS Newsletter", and it was my first real foray into public writing. I didn’t have a journalism degree. I wasn’t a communications officer. I was a youthful Finance and Admin Assistant with a spreadsheet in one hand and a deadline in the other. My official job was budgets, logistics, and documents—but somehow, I volunteered (or was gently cornered) into running a bi-monthly bulletin that later morphed into a monthly feature.
It targeted community development practitioners and grassroots organizations—the kind of folk doing hard work in hard places, often with little thanks. People I admired. Which made it worse. Because every time I sat down to draft an issue, I felt like a fraud trying to speak to giants.
The pressure to sound “professional” and “informed” while also being timely and relevant… well, let’s just say, imposter syndrome and I became very close. I’d start with ideas that felt strong, then second-guess every sentence.
I'd wrestle with tone. Should I be formal? Warm? Data-heavy? Light and chatty?
Sometimes, I’d panic-write entire paragraphs that felt more like internal memos than community updates. Other times, I missed the soft editorial deadline because I was knee-deep in an audit file or chasing a procurement form that had mysteriously vanished between departments.

But you know what? I learned.
I learned how to coax ideas out of thin air and package them into digestible stories. I learned how to listen—really listen—to what field officers, coordinators, and beneficiaries were saying during check-ins, then weave those insights into something others could read and nod at. I learned how to breathe life into statistics, how to ask better questions, and most of all, how to hit “Send” even when I wasn’t sure if it was good enough.

That humble little newsletter gave me my first glimpse into the power of writing with purpose. It wasn’t about being perfect—it was about showing up. And now, many years later, I find myself doing that again, but on my own terms.

Welcome to this blog—the curiously breezy side of my journey.

Here, I’m letting go of the pressure to sound like I have all the answers. I’m trading the polished newsletter tone for something a little more human. A space where I can reflect, explore, and sometimes ramble about the intersection of finance, technology, creativity, and life in motion. A place where I can ask the questions I used to bury under meeting minutes and grant reports.

If you’ve ever felt like a beginner in your own story—whether in a new role, a new field, or a new version of yourself—then pull up a chair. I’ve been there. I still am.

This blog is for the curious. For the brave. For the quietly ambitious and the not-so-sure. It’s a continuation of a journey that began with grassroots voices and formatting battles in MS Publisher. And it’s just getting interesting.

So… karibu. Let’s keep this breeze moving.

From Compliance to Code

A curious detour through Finance, Donors, and Dreams...

Let me tell you a secret: the life of a traditional finance professional in a donor-funded organization is not as quiet as the spreadsheet might suggest.

In fact, it's a chaotic dance between deadlines and documentation. One moment you're reconciling the petty cash ledger, and the next, you're responding to a donor query from three months ago about why a payment voucher is missing a signature. It sounds mundane until you realize that these documents—hard or soft copy, scanned in triplicate, sometimes even notarized—are the bread and butter of continued funding.

I spent years in these trenches.

________________________________________

The World of Multi-Donor Projects
Working with multi-donor funded organizations meant living in audit-mode by default. Supporting documentation wasn’t just “important”—it was gospel. Every payment, whether for salaries, travel, or training refreshments, needed proof. Timesheets. Receipts. Quotations. Signed approvals. And when those weren't airtight, the dreaded review queries would land. Monthly.

Payroll wasn’t a simple disbursement—it was a careful choreography of statutory deductions (PAYE, NHIF/SHIF, NSSF), approvals, tax submissions, and reconciliations. Burn-rate calculations needed to be up-to-date to inform budget variance analyses and donor reporting. The dreaded underspending or overspending alerts? Yes, those too.

Procurement wasn’t a run to the shop. It was a process—soliciting three quotes, reviewing for fairness, evaluating against policy, justifying selections. All while ensuring internal controls weren’t just “in place,” but actively respected. You learned to implement policy, but also tweak it when reality punched.

And then came HR. Reviewing timesheets, approving leave, supporting staff travel, and sorting accommodation logistics for visiting consultants or program beneficiaries. Sometimes you'd find yourself managing the company car logbook in the morning and answering a finance policy query by lunch.

It was everything, everywhere, all at once.

But within that intensity, there was growth. You became a cross-functional operator. You led when needed, adapted when required, and met your update meetings with a mix of exhaustion and pride. You learned how organizations actually breathe. You saw how leadership isn’t just in the title—it’s in keeping the engine running behind the scenes.

________________________________________

Shifting Gears: Welcome to Construction and Commerce
When I moved into commercial enterprises—specifically construction and engineering—the world tilted.

Gone were donor frameworks and burn-rates. Here, the goal was profitability. Every decision had a price tag, and that tag had to be justified through a business lens, not a development one.

Tax obligations evolved—suddenly, VAT returns and withholding tax compliance took center stage. Ownership structures became more concentrated. Approvals were faster, but the pressure to deliver was tighter. Donor goodwill was replaced by investor/owner expectations.
You didn’t have quarterly review meetings—you had directors asking why the site delayed delivery by a day and what it meant for cashflow.

And yet, my donor-funded background helped. My processes were airtight. My compliance game strong. My ability to handle audits, deadlines, and documentation gave me an edge. What they needed was order. What I had was just that—seasoned, stress-tested, and systems-oriented.

________________________________________

Now Entering: Tech Territory
Then came a deeper nudge. A feeling. A shift.

The world was changing. Digital transformation wasn’t just a buzzword anymore—it was the new minimum. And I knew that if I wanted to future-proof my career, I couldn’t sit in yesterday’s tools while tomorrow’s tech left me behind.

So here I am. A finance and administration professional, knee-deep in HTML and Python, teaching myself how to build AI-powered financial tools while figuring out how to pay the bills in the meantime.

And it’s terrifying.

Not because the tech world is unkind—but because the terrain is unfamiliar. I don’t have a Computer Science degree. I don’t speak “backend” fluently yet. And transitioning as an older professional, especially when the job market is flooded with younger, tech-native talent, can feel like starting from scratch in a language you’re still learning to pronounce.

It’s even harder when you add context: USAID and other donor fund cuts mean fewer opportunities in the non-profit sector, more competition, and tighter belts across the board. Many of us with years of experience are now fishing in the same small pond, wondering if it’s time to switch lakes altogether.

But here’s the thing: while I may not be “tech-born,” I’m problem-tested. I bring years of financial insight, organizational discipline, and compliance-driven thinking—things even the best developers sometimes overlook. I’m not here to replace the coder—I’m here to complement them, to bring structure, financial management and understanding into a world full of innovation but sometimes short on operational grounding.

________________________________________

So, Why This Story?
Because somewhere out there is someone like me—maybe mid-career, maybe at a crossroads—wondering if it’s too late to pivot, to learn, to explore. It’s not. The trick is to let your past become your springboard, not your ceiling.

Finance taught me about systems. Donors taught me about accountability. Construction taught me about performance. And now, tech is teaching me about reinvention.

We don’t need to abandon our history. We just need to remix it for the future.

I hope this piece reminded you that growth isn’t linear. It’s layered.
